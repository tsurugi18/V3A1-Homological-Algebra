\par\textbf{Exercise 1}

For any map $\varphi,\psi$ where compositions $f\circ \varphi,f\circ\psi$ make sense, we have if $f$ is injective then $f\circ\phi=f\circ\psi$ implies $\phi=\psi$ as maps between sets. Thus the morphism $f\circ$ is injective. Similarly, as $g$ is surjective $\circ g$ is an injective morphism. For the exactness as $g\circ f = 0$ the inclusion,
\begin{equation*}
    \image  f\circ \subseteq \ker g\circ
\end{equation*}
is clear. If $\varphi:X\to B\in\ker g\circ$ then $\image\varphi\subseteq\ker g = \image f$. Thus we can assume $B=\image f$, namely $f$ is bijective. Set $\varphi' = f^{-1}\circ\varphi$. This is in $\Hom_{\Ab}(X,A)$ and $f\circ \varphi' = f\circ f^{-1}\circ\varphi = \varphi$. 
\par For the other case, by similar argument, we have,
\begin{equation*}
    \image \circ g \subseteq\ker \circ f.
\end{equation*}
Let $\psi\in\ker\circ f$. Similarly, $\ker \psi \supseteq\image f = \ker g$. Thus we consider $B/\ker g$ instead of $g$. This make $g$ bijective, therefore set $\psi' = \psi\circ g^{-1}\in\Hom_{\Ab}(C,X)$.
\par\textbf{Exercise 3}
We have a commutative diagram,
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzcd}
\cdots \arrow[r] & A_k \arrow[r, "d^A_k"] \arrow[d, "f_k"] & A_{k-1} \arrow[r] \arrow[d, "f_{k-1}"] & \cdots \\
\cdots \arrow[r] & B_k \arrow[r, "d_k^B"']                 & B_{k-1} \arrow[r]                      & \cdots
\end{tikzcd}
\end{center}
From Exercise 3 of Week 0 sheet, we have,
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzcd}
\ker d_k^A \arrow[r] \arrow[d, "f_k'"] & A_k \arrow[r, "d_k"] \arrow[d, "f_k"] & A_{k-1} \arrow[r] \arrow[d, "f_{k-1}"] & \coker d_k^A \arrow[d, "f_{k-1}'"] \\
\ker d_k^B \arrow[r]                   & B_k \arrow[r, "d_k"']                 & B_{k-1} \arrow[r]                      & \coker d_k^B                      
\end{tikzcd}
\end{center}
Thus the map $H_n(f):H_n(A_\bullet)\to H_n(B_\bullet)$ is given by restricting the domain of $\coker d_{k+1}^A\to\coker d_{k+1}^B$ to $H_n(A_\bullet)$.
\par Consider, $f_\bullet:A_\bullet\to B_\bullet$ such that
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzcd}
A_\bullet : &0 \arrow[r] \arrow[d] & \Z \arrow[r, "\times 2"] \arrow[d, "\times 0"] & \Z \arrow[r] \arrow[d, "\mod{2}"] & 0 \arrow[d] \\
B_\bullet : &0 \arrow[r]           & 0 \arrow[r, hook]                              & \Z/2\Z \arrow[r]                  & 0          
\end{tikzcd}
\end{center}
Then
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzcd}
0 \arrow[r] \arrow[d] & \Z/2\Z \arrow[r] \arrow[d] & 0 \arrow[d] \\
0 \arrow[r]           & \Z/2\Z \arrow[r]           & 0          
\end{tikzcd}
\end{center}
In the construction of $H_\bullet(f)$ in the beginning, $\Z/2\Z\to\Z/2\Z$ is not non-zero thus an isomorphism. Since the only possible group homomorphism from $\Z/2\Z\to\Z$ is $0$. Thus if $H_\bullet(f)$ is quasi-isomorphism, there wouldn't be any quasi-inverse.
